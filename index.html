Some Theory Research Projects from My Undergraduate Studies

Improving Network Fairness With Edge Addition

Assuming information spreads along the edges of a social network, some nodes naturally have greater access to information simply because of their position in the network, creating inherent advantage gaps. The problem I studied asks whether we can add a limited number of edges to reduce these gaps (in particular, to equalize the closeness centralities of two given nodes). This turns out to be NP-hard, so my work focused on designing approximation algorithms and proving inapproximability results.

This research formed my undergraduate thesis at Haverford, where I was fortunate to be advised by Dr. Sorelle Friedler (CS) and Dr. Josh Sabloff (Math), and to collaborate with Dr. Blair Sullivan and the Theory in Practice group at the University of Utah. My Math thesis submission is accessible to a general math audience, while the CS thesis submission has a more extensive literature review and assumes familiarity with concepts from an undergraduate algorithms course.

Improving Quickselect With Dynamic Pivot Selection

The Quickselect algorithm efficiently finds the kth largest element in an unsorted list, running in expected linear time. However, its performance depends heavily on selecting good “pivots” during recursion, elements that ideally split the list roughly in half and minimize the size of the subproblem. In this project, I proposed and implemented a dynamic pivot selection strategy, and compared this approach to Quickselect's standard method of choosing pivots at random, evaluating both on large random datasets.

My method produced more balanced partitions than Quickselect in practice, while remaining far simpler and more implementable than the state-of-the-art Floyd-Rivest algorithm. I summarized my findings in this technical article, and was advised by Dr. Amrinder Arora of George Washington University.

Comparing Bit and Time Efficiency of Coding Algorithms on Real World Data

I analyzed the performance of three data compression algorithms (fixed-length encoding, Shannon coding, and Huffman coding) using real-world text data. To evaluate bit efficiency, I examined how the entropy of each scheme changed as the number of unique characters increased. For this, I wrote a program that generated large alphabets by using hexadecimal representations, allowing me to test tens of thousands of unique characters instead of being limited to the hundreds of characters available in ASCII.

I also evaluated time efficiency by measuring how quickly each scheme could encode and decode messages. The results confirmed that Huffman consistently outperformed Shannon in practice, while fixed-length encoding was less efficient overall, even in cases where our theoretical analysis suggested it might compete with Shannon coding.

A Greedy Algorithm For Registrar Scheduling

In this project, we designed a greedy algorithm to simulate the scheduling process a college registrar performs -- assigning rooms, professors, times, and students to classes without conflicts. The algorithm ensures correctness (e.g., no double-bookings or schedule clashes) and incorporates realistic constraints, such as department-specific rooms, minimizing course overlap within a major, and accommodating popular classes over Zoom. We also analyzed the algorithm's time complexity, and ran experiments to test how well it satisfied student preferences -- on average over 80% of students were placed into their preferred classes while handling complex scheduling constraints.
